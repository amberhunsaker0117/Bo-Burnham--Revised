Performativity
This project was originally created for a doctoral level Rhetorical Theory course. Leaning into Burnham's heavy critique of performativity I sent my professor (Dr. Danielle Nielsen) 3D glasses for the "All Eyes On Me (Outtakes)" lyrics.

The glasses were put into an authentic Chanel glasses case, wrapped in an Amazon gift bag, in an Amazon box with original Amazon tape (all to paradoxically lean into the anti-capitalistic narrative Burnham presents and to play with the irony found within the “Bezos’s” songs).

When I sent Dr. Nielsen the package, I also sent her an email (see below) with the tracking number. Like everything else in this project, it was also over the top. (The attachment from the email is the same gif found on the home page of this site.)
I loved the fact this is email looks like a spam email. My professor knew a package was coming, since I had to ask for a good mailing address. But sending the email made the entire experience even more ridiculous and campy.
In regards to putting together this entire website, part of the performativity is using gifs and images from Burnham’s Inside to speak for me, or say things in a way that are better said in images. If pictures are worth a 1000 words, I wonder how many words gifs are worth? Particularly in web-text based writings, like here in Kairos, the insertion of videos, pictures, and gifs add to the argument. Not only can we use them for evidence and examples, but to really use them to bring connotations that text alone cannot. The “Ugh face/Feel Good” meme in the “Bezos II” lyrics is a prime example. Those versed in “meme-lore” understand the deep-seeded irony of that meme. Those who are not, either will learn, or are not as affected by it, but can still pick up the meaning.

Bo Burnham’s Inside can only be found online. To turn around and satirically berate viewers for being chronically online, the exact place where his audience finds, watches, and supports his art, is bold. I can only hope to be such a badass.
